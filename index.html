How LandsITrack Works

This system monitors two sensors installed in the ground, a tilt sensor and a soil moisture sensor. Together, they detect early signs of a possible landslide and automatically alert the right people, so communities can react quickly and safely, before disaster strikes.

Why We Monitor Landslides
üèòÔ∏è
The Problem with Landslides
Landslides are one of the most dangerous natural disasters in mountainous communities, and one of the most silent. Unlike typhoons or floods, they often give very little visible warning before they happen. The ground can look completely normal one moment and collapse the next.

In many barangays, communities live on or near slopes. After heavy rains, the soil slowly weakens and shifts, sometimes for hours or days before anything is visible on the surface. By the time cracks appear or the ground starts moving noticeably, it may already be too late to evacuate safely.

This is exactly the problem LandsITrack is built to solve. By placing sensors directly in the ground, the system can detect invisible early movement, the kind that happens underground before anything shows on the surface, and alert the right people with enough time to act.
‚è±Ô∏è Early warning is the difference between a safe evacuation and a tragedy. LandsITrack gives communities that extra time.
What the Sensors Measure ‚Äî and Why
üìê
Tilt Angle
When the ground is stable, the sensor rod stands straight up at 0¬∞. If the ground beneath it starts to shift or slide, the sensor leans with it, and that lean is measured in degrees.

Even a small tilt, like 5¬∞, can be a significant early warning sign that the soil is beginning to move underground. The larger the angle, the further the ground has shifted.
ü™® Think of it like planting a stick straight into a hillside. If the ground moves, the stick leans. LandsITrack measures exactly how far it leans, automatically and in real time.

Why we measure tilt: Ground movement is the most direct sign of a slope becoming unstable. The tilt sensor captures movement at the source, deep in the ground, before it becomes visible at the surface. It is the clearest, most direct evidence that a landslide may be forming.
üíß
Soil Moisture
The soil moisture sensor measures how much water has soaked into the soil, as a percentage from 0% (dry) to 100% (completely saturated). When soil absorbs too much rainwater, it becomes heavier and loses its internal friction, the grip that holds soil particles together on a slope.

Wet, heavy, slippery soil is far more likely to slide than dry, firm soil.
üåßÔ∏è Think of dry sand vs. wet sand. Dry sand holds. Wet sand flows. The same principle applies to hillside soil after heavy rain, at a certain point, water overwhelms the soil's ability to stay in place.

Why we measure moisture: Most landslides in tropical regions are triggered by rainfall. Water accumulates in the soil over hours, slowly weakening it. By monitoring moisture continuously, LandsITrack can catch this weakening process early, even before the ground starts moving, giving even more advance warning.
How the Two Sensors Work Together
üîó
A Complete Picture of Risk
Neither sensor alone tells the full story. Tilt without moisture could mean the ground shifted long ago and is stable now. Moisture without tilt could mean the soil is wet but still holding. But when both sensors show elevated readings at the same time, wet soil that is also starting to tilt, that combination is a strong signal that a slope is actively failing.

This is why LandsITrack uses both together to calculate an alert level. The system checks both readings on every update and takes the worse of the two as the basis for the alert.
üß† Two sensors watching the same slope, day and night, rain or shine, that's the core of why LandsITrack works as an early warning system.
